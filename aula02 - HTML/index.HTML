AN IQ TOO low?

The concept of “low IQ” is often misunderstood and oversimplified. IQ, or intelligence quotient, is a score derived from standardized tests designed to measure certain cognitive abilities such as reasoning, memory, and problem-solving. However, reducing a person’s overall intelligence or worth to a single number can be misleading and unfair.
People with lower IQ scores may face challenges in academic or highly analytical environments, but that does not mean they lack intelligence altogether. Human ability is complex and multifaceted. Skills like creativity, emotional understanding, social awareness, practical problem-solving, and resilience are not always captured by IQ tests. In many real-world situations, these qualities can be just as important—if not more so—than abstract reasoning skills.
It is also important to consider external factors that influence IQ scores. Access to education, socioeconomic background, nutrition, and cultural differences can all impact performance on standardized tests. What might appear as a “low IQ” result could reflect limited opportunities rather than an individual’s true potential.
Labeling individuals based on IQ can lead to stigma and lowered expectations, which may further limit their growth. A more constructive approach is to focus on strengths, provide supportive learning environments, and recognize that intelligence develops over time. With the right encouragement and resources, people can improve their skills and contribute meaningfully to society in diverse ways.
Ultimately, intelligence is not a single fixed trait. It is a dynamic combination of abilities, experiences, and personal qualities that cannot be fully captured by a number alone.

THE AVAREGE IQ

Average IQ: 85 to 115 (about 68% of the population falls within this range).

HOW IQ INTERFER IN REAL LIFE?

Intelligence is often seen as the ultimate measure of a person’s potential. Parents brag about their children’s IQ scores, schools sort students based on test results, and society tends to place those with high intelligence on a pedestal. But does a high IQ really determine your success in life — or is the story more complex? This question isn’t just theoretical. It’s deeply personal to many — especially those who grow up under the shadow of a “gifted” sibling.

The Pressure of High Expectations

For some families, intelligence is everything. One father, obsessed with IQ scores, held his younger son Charles — the so-called genius of the family — as the gold standard. Diagnosed as gifted, Charles was reading by age two, speaking multiple languages by age three, and had an IQ over 145. Meanwhile, his older sibling (the narrator of our story) was left behind emotionally. Despite once sharing a close bond with their father, everything changed after Charles’s talents emerged. Even small academic setbacks led to sarcastic remarks, such as being compared to an ape with an IQ of 40. This emotional neglect left scars. It’s a story many can relate to — where intelligence becomes a measure not only of capability, but of love, attention, and self-worth.
